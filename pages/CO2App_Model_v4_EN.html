Calculation Model Documentation (v4)

This documentation is divided into two parts: Model (this page) and Values & References (advanced extension).

For the complete table of constants and references, see: Values & References.

1. Core principles

  • The carbon footprint is calculated as the sum of individual categories: Housing + Transport + Food + Lifestyle + Digital + Social.
  • Units are expressed in kg CO₂/year (and converted to t CO₂/year where appropriate).
  • The reference target for a sustainable lifestyle is 2.5 tCO₂/person/year, in line with the Paris Agreement goal for limiting warming to 1.5°C.
  • The calculations mainly refer to Greece in 2025, particularly with respect to the carbon intensity of the electricity grid (Carbon Intensity).
  • For the Housing category, calculations are normalised to a reference dwelling size of 100 m², enabling meaningful comparisons between households.
Note: CO2App does not attempt a complete and detailed accounting of all individual CO₂ emissions of the student–user, a process that would be time‑consuming, exhausting, and often unreliable in an educational context. Instead, the application aims to classify users into representative behavioural categories (housing, transport, diet, consumption, etc.), which correspond to typical emission levels derived from international and national empirical data. The model relies on documented average values and coefficients, while intentionally introducing simplifications to enhance didactic clarity and comparability of results. In this way, a degree of numerical accuracy is deliberately sacrificed in favour of understanding the key mechanisms linking everyday choices to carbon emissions.
Scenario exploration: The application allows the modification of key model constants (such as energy‑related coefficients or reference assumptions), enabling users —and especially educators— to explore alternative scenarios, compare assumptions, and study how individual parameters affect the overall carbon footprint.

2. Mathematical structure

For each activity, the general relationship applies: Emissions = Activity × Factor.

2.1 Total footprint

CO2_total = CO2_housing + CO2_transport + CO2_food + CO2_goods + CO2_digital + CO2_social

3. Housing

  • Thermal demand: expressed in kWh/m²·year and based on reference climatic assumptions for Southern Greece.
  • Electricity consumption: approximated using average reference values from national and European statistical sources.
  • Heat pump: its efficiency is not explicitly shown in the user interface but is embedded in the corresponding model coefficients.

4. Transport

  • Private vehicles: emission factors in kg CO₂/km for gasoline, diesel, EVs, and motorcycles.
  • EVs: their emissions are not considered zero and depend on the carbon intensity of the electricity grid (Carbon Intensity), which is a core model parameter.
  • Public transport: distinction between Metro and Bus.
  • Flights: educational reference distances (e.g. 450 km, 1600 km).

5. Food

This category is expressed as a multiplier relative to a “classic” diet (1.0):

  • Vegan: 0.5
  • Vegetarian: 0.65
  • Mediterranean: 0.85
  • Classic: 1.0
  • Beef‑heavy: 1.4

6. Goods & Consumption (Lifestyle)

  • The slider is educational in nature and is documented only for: 0.4, 1.0, 2.2.

7. Digital footprint

  • The digital footprint is not among the dominant emission factors; however, it is included deliberately for educational purposes.
  • Its aim is to stimulate discussion on the fact that greenhouse‑gas emissions are also linked to “intangible” activities, such as the use of digital services, data storage, AI usage, and online consumption (social media).

8. Social / shared footprint

  • The social or shared footprint refers to emissions associated with the operation of collective infrastructures and public services (e.g. healthcare, education, transport, administration).
  • It is not the result of individual consumer choices but of collective decisions and social priorities.
  • Its inclusion highlights the democratic responsibility of citizens to seek its reduction through social and political engagement, rather than solely through individual consumption choices.
